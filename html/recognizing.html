Black bears (Ursus americanus) are the only wild species of bear in the eastern United States.

We encourage you to keep your senses alert, especially as you are hiking and collecting data. Do not attempt to approach, feed, or harass wildlife.


Familiarize yourself with black bear signs, and what to do if you come across a bear in the wild:

How to identify black bear signs:

Black Bear Other Animals

 Animal   

 Black body with brown/tan muzzle; stubby tail; occasionally a white patch on chest

Long tail

 Adults are 3-6' in length from nose to tail; 100-500 lbs

  Hair   

 Approx. 1-4" long; guard hairs are all black; underfur may be brown

 Shed hair may be lighter from sun exposure

 Hair from raccoon, oppossum, and deer have multiple other colors (white, cream, tan) on a strand.

 Commonly found on ground, tree trunks, barbed wired

 Tracks   

 Broad footprints, about 4-7" long, with 5 toes and sometimes claw marks

 Found on trails, in soft soil, mud, or sand

 Scat

 Usually dark brown (varies by diet), ~1-2" diameter; cylindrical and sometimes coiled

 If diet was mainly berries, may be soft and liquidy

 Content may include grasses, seeds, animal hair, shells

What to do if you see a black bear:

(adapted from the book, "Living With Bears" by Linda Masterson and the Be Bear Aware Program)
  1. Stay calm.
  2. Do not run.
  3. Avoid eye contact with the bear.
  4. Facing the bear, back away slowly. Alter your hiking route.
  5. If the bear continues to approach, make noise - scream, yell, blow your whistle. (But do not squeal or sound like a scared or injured prey!)
  6. Bells have been said to not be effective. Do not leave food out in the open. Bear spray can be a good thing to carry. Make sure you know how use it properly.
